A vector embedding is a numerical representation of the meaning (semantics) and relationship
of some data, such as text, an image, or audio so that a model can understand and compare it.

Basically -> embedding is a way to convey the "meaning" of a word to a model

Suppose you have these sentences:

I love dogs.
Dogs are amazing animals.
I enjoy pizza.

An embedding model converts them into vectors (lists of numbers):

"I love dogs."
    ↓
[0.13, -0.82, 0.45, 1.72, ...]

"Dogs are amazing animals."
            ↓
[0.10, -0.80, 0.48, 1.69, ...]

"I enjoy pizza."
        ↓
[-1.23, 2.14, -0.61, 0.05, ...]

Notice that the first two vectors are numerically closer to each other than the third.
!!!!    That closeness represents semantic similarity.     !!!!

*****************************************************************************************************************

Why do we need them?

Suppose you ask: "Tell me about canines."

Your database contains: Dogs are loyal pets.

Keyword search might fail because: canines ≠ dogs

!!!! But embeddings capture meaning. !!!!

The embedding for:
canines

will be close to the embedding for:
dogs

So the system can retrieve the correct document.

! how is tokenisation different from embedding !        -       bounty question

One-line difference
Tokenization → Converts text into token IDs (identifiers).
Embeddings → Converts token IDs into dense vectors that capture semantic meaning.

tokens are just identifiers for a given word/input
it is basically an ID given to that particular word
eg:

Input: I love dogs

Tokenizer produces token IDs:       It's simply the ID assigned to that token.
"I"      → 40
" love"  → 1842
" dogs"  → 9021

Now each token ID is looked up in an embedding table.

For example:

40
↓
[0.23, -0.51, 1.22, ...]

1842
 ↓
[-0.72, 0.11, 0.98, ...]

9021
 ↓
[1.63, -0.44, 0.09, ...]

Now the model has vectors instead of IDs.

These vectors contain semantic information.

////////////////////////////////////////////////

An embedding is more like a feature vector.

Dog
↓
[
  mammal,
  animal,
  pet,
  furry,
  four-legged,
  ...
]

Embeddings:

dog → [0.12, 0.87, ...]
cat → [0.14, 0.84, ...]
car → [-1.23, 2.56, ...]

Now:

Dog and Cat vectors are close.
Dog and Car vectors are far apart.

The numbers encode meaning, not identity.

Q. but i can have multiple meanings of a word right?
like canine can mean a dog and my tooth too, how does an embedding capture this?

The answer depends on which kind of embeddings we're talking about.
Modern LLMs use contextual embeddings.

The embedding is computed using the entire sentence, not just the word itself.

Sentence 1:
The canine is barking.

The embedding for "canine" becomes something like:

close to:
    dog
    wolf
    pet
animal

------

Sentence 2:
The canine tooth is broken.

Now the embedding for "canine" changes:

close to:
    tooth
    dentist
    jaw
    molar

-----

Same word. Different vector.

----------------------------------------------------------

When computing the representation of "canine", the transformer doesn't look only at that word.
It also looks at surrounding words - the entire sentence.

This is one of the biggest reasons Transformers outperformed older models.

Older models:
    canine
      ↓
    One vector forever

Transformers:
    Sentence
        ↓
    Understand context
        ↓
    Generate a context-specific vector
